Split AC Installation Guide 2025 — Everything You Need to Know

A split AC is only as good as its installation. India's AC market sells millions of units every year, but a significant percentage develop problems within 1–2 years — and most of those problems trace back to poor installation: wrong copper pipe sizing, improper flaring, no vacuum pull, incorrect drainage slope, or outdoor unit placed in a heat trap. This guide explains exactly how proper split AC installation works, what corners are cut by cheap installers, and how to protect your investment.

Split AC Components: What Gets Installed

  • Indoor unit (evaporator) — wall-mounted; contains the evaporator coil, fan motor, filter, and PCB
  • Outdoor unit (condenser) — external; contains the compressor, condenser coil, and fan
  • Copper refrigerant pipes — connect indoor to outdoor; carry refrigerant gas
  • Drain pipe — carries condensate water from indoor unit outside
  • Electrical cable — power and signal between indoor and outdoor units
  • Wall bracket — holds the outdoor unit; must be load-rated

The Correct Installation Process (Step by Step)

1

Heat Load Calculation & Unit Placement Survey

Before drilling a single hole, the installer should assess room size, sun exposure, ceiling height, glass area, and heat sources to confirm the AC tonnage is correct. They also decide the best position for indoor and outdoor units — minimising copper pipe length and ensuring the outdoor unit has adequate airflow clearance.

2

Indoor Unit Mounting

A horizontal mounting plate is fixed to the wall using masonry anchors (not plastic plugs). The indoor unit should be at least 15cm from the ceiling, centred in the room where possible, and mounted level (drain must slope toward the drainage hole). A core hole is drilled through the wall for the pipe run.

3

Copper Pipe Cutting, Bending & Flaring

This is the most critical step. The correct gauge copper pipe for the AC tonnage is cut to length, bent gently without kinking, and flared at both ends using a proper flaring tool. Flaring creates the leak-proof cone joint. Bad flaring = gas leak. Pipes must be insulated with closed-cell insulation to prevent condensation and heat gain.

4

Outdoor Unit Bracket & Mounting

The bracket must be rated for the outdoor unit's weight (typically 35–55kg). It should be fixed into RCC or brick — not just plaster. Anti-vibration pads reduce noise. Clearance: minimum 30cm on all sides, 60cm at the discharge face. Never install in an enclosed space or facing a wall.

5

Pipe Connection & Pressure Testing

The flared pipes are connected to the indoor and outdoor unit valves and tightened to torque spec (not guesswork). The system is then pressure tested with dry nitrogen at 300–600 PSI for 30 minutes to check for leaks before refrigerant is introduced.

6

Vacuum Pull (Evacuation)

A vacuum pump removes all air and moisture from the copper pipe and indoor unit coil. This step is non-negotiable — moisture inside refrigerant lines causes compressor failure within 2–5 years. Vacuum must be pulled to at least 500 microns and held for 15–30 minutes. Many cheap installers skip this entirely.

7

Refrigerant Charge & Commissioning

The outdoor unit's service valves are opened to release factory-charged refrigerant. If additional gas is needed (for long pipe runs), the correct amount is added using a manifold gauge. The technician checks suction and discharge pressure, supply air temperature, and current draw to confirm the system is working correctly.

8

Drain Pipe & Electrical Connections

The drain pipe must slope consistently downward from the indoor unit to the outside — no dips or upward sections. Electrical connections are made with correct wire gauge, properly insulated, with the outdoor unit earthed. A dedicated 16A circuit with MCB is required for 1.5-ton ACs.

Red flag: If your installation takes less than 2 hours, corners were cut. A proper 1.5-ton split AC installation by skilled technicians takes 3–4 hours including pressure testing and vacuum pull.

Signs of Bad AC Installation (Catch These Early)

🔴 AC not cooling adequately from day 1

The unit was installed without checking refrigerant charge. The factory charge may have been released before connecting pipes (a common shortcut), leaving the system undercharged.

🔴 Water dripping from the indoor unit indoors

The drain pipe was not sloped correctly, is blocked, or is too short. Condensate is backing up into the unit and overflowing onto the wall or floor.

🔴 Outdoor unit vibrating loudly

The bracket is not level, anti-vibration pads are missing, or the unit is mounted on a surface that amplifies resonance. Can cause compressor damage over time.

🔴 Gas leak within 6–12 months

Almost always caused by improper flaring (bad flare joint) or pipes that were not torqued correctly. The leak develops slowly as vibration works the joint loose.

🔴 Condensation on copper pipes (sweating)

Insulation on copper pipes was not fitted, was damaged, or was too thin. Pipe sweating drips onto walls, causing mould and water damage.

🔴 Compressor failure in 2–4 years

Often caused by moisture entering the system due to skipped vacuum pull. Moisture reacts with refrigerant to form acid, which destroys compressor bearings and windings.

What to Check After Installation

  1. AC cools the room to the set temperature within 20–30 minutes on a hot day
  2. No water dripping from the indoor unit inside the room
  3. Drain pipe is dripping water outside (confirms drainage working)
  4. Outdoor unit fan is spinning; compressor is running (hum, not rattle)
  5. Copper pipes have insulation that is intact and dry
  6. No smell of burning from the indoor unit
  7. Remote control operates all functions correctly
  8. Indoor unit mounted level (use a phone spirit level app)

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does split AC installation take?
A proper split AC installation by skilled technicians takes 3–4 hours. This includes mounting both units, running copper pipes, pressure testing, pulling vacuum, and commissioning. If told it takes 1–1.5 hours, corners are being cut.
What copper pipe size is needed for 1.5-ton AC?
A 1.5-ton split AC requires 1/4 inch (6.35mm) liquid line and 3/8 inch (9.52mm) suction line copper pipes. Using undersized pipe reduces efficiency. Using oversized pipe wastes refrigerant charge. Always use the pipe sizes specified in the AC's installation manual.
Can I install AC on my own?
No. Handling refrigerant requires certification in India. Incorrect installation voids the manufacturer warranty. Without proper tools (flaring tool, vacuum pump, manifold gauge), you cannot do a correct installation. Always use a certified HVAC technician.
What is AC vacuum pull and why is it important?
Vacuum pull uses a vacuum pump to remove all air and moisture from the copper pipes before refrigerant is introduced. Moisture inside refrigerant lines reacts with refrigerant to form hydrofluoric acid, which destroys the compressor within 2–5 years. Skipping this step is one of the most common and damaging installation shortcuts.
How far can the indoor and outdoor unit be from each other?
Most 1–2 ton split ACs support pipe runs of 5–15 meters without additional refrigerant charge. Beyond 7.5 meters (standard factory charge), additional refrigerant must be added. Maximum distance varies by model — check the installation manual. Very long pipe runs (15m+) reduce efficiency.
Where should the outdoor unit NOT be installed?
Never install the outdoor unit: in an enclosed space with no airflow, directly facing a wall with less than 30cm clearance, in direct intense sunlight with no shade (reduces efficiency), above a gas line or electrical panel, or in a location where hot discharge air recirculates back into the unit.
